Understanding the Nutritional Demands of Altitude Training
When athletes venture into mountainous terrain for training, they face a unique set of physiological and nutritional challenges. The impact of altitude on calorie needs is a critical aspect that must be addressed to ensure optimal performance and health. Here, we delve into the specifics of altitude nutrition, highlighting key considerations for mountain athletes.
Physiological Changes at Altitude
Training at altitude introduces several physiological changes that affect nutritional needs. One of the primary concerns is the increase in resting metabolic rate (RMR) and energy expenditure. Studies have shown that at moderate altitudes (around 2,200-2,400 meters), athletes experience a modest increase in metabolism, which can lead to increased energy requirements.
At higher elevations, the body’s ability to adapt to the lower oxygen levels (hypoxia) results in increased carbohydrate oxidation during exercise. This means that athletes need to consume more carbohydrates to meet the fuel requirements of their training.
Energy Intake and Calorie Needs
Athletes training at altitude need to significantly increase their energy intake to compensate for the higher energy expenditure. A study involving elite endurance athletes found that energy intake increased by 35% during a 3-week training camp at 2,320 meters. This increase was largely due to the consumption of snacks, which accounted for 70% of the additional energy intake.
To maintain energy balance, athletes should focus on calorie-dense foods. Homemade granola, energy bites, smoothies, and protein shakes are excellent options. Eating small, frequent meals and snacks is also recommended to ensure adequate nutrition.
Carbohydrate Requirements
Carbohydrates are the body’s preferred energy source, especially during exercise at altitude. The elevated levels of hormones such as adrenaline, noradrenaline, and cortisol increase the use of carbohydrates for fuel. Athletes should aim to derive at least 60% of their calories from carbohydrates. Foods like bread, rice, pasta, tortillas, fruit, potatoes, and whole grains such as oats and barley are essential components of a high-altitude diet.
In addition to solid foods, carbohydrate-rich beverages like smoothies, fruit juices, and sports hydration beverages can help boost carbohydrate and fluid intake.
Protein and Muscle Maintenance
Protein is crucial for muscle repair and maintenance, particularly after intense workouts at altitude. Athletes should include protein-rich foods such as lean meats, eggs, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, and protein bars and powders in their diet. Ensuring adequate protein intake helps prevent muscle tissue loss, which can occur due to the increased metabolic rate and energy deficit at altitude.
Hydration and Fluid Intake
Adequate hydration is vital at altitude due to the dry air and increased ventilation. Athletes need to consume at least 3-5 liters of hydrating beverages per day. This can include plain water, fruit juice, smoothies, protein drinks, milk, and milk alternatives, as well as decaffeinated tea. Sports beverages with electrolytes are particularly beneficial for meeting carbohydrate, electrolyte, and fluid goals.
Iron and Antioxidant Requirements
Iron is a critical micronutrient at altitude, as it supports the increase in red blood cell count and hemoglobin mass, which are essential for aerobic power. Athletes should have their iron levels monitored and follow an optimal iron supplementation protocol if necessary. Iron-rich foods such as fortified grains, leafy green vegetables like spinach and kale, beans, lentils, beef, and eggs should be included in the diet.
Antioxidant-rich foods are also important, as they help mitigate oxidative stress without interfering with training adaptations. Foods high in antioxidants, rather than high-dose single antioxidant supplements, are recommended for their health benefits and lack of negative impact on training responses.

– **Macronutrient Balance**: Focus on carbohydrates, include sufficient protein, and monitor iron levels.

Real-World Examples and Case Studies
A study involving 31 elite endurance athletes during a 3-week altitude training camp at 2,320 meters demonstrated that these athletes increased their energy intake significantly, with a notable increase in carbohydrate consumption. The provision of antioxidant-rich snacks improved the dietary composition but had minimal impact on nutrition-related blood parameters.
Another example is the practical application of nutrition guidelines in high-altitude alpinism. Here, the emphasis is on maintaining energy balance through carbohydrate prioritization and ensuring protein balance, especially during different stages of an expedition.
Conclusion and Next Steps
Training at altitude presents unique nutritional challenges that athletes must address to optimize their performance and health. By understanding the physiological changes at altitude and adjusting their diet accordingly, athletes can better adapt to the demands of high-altitude training.

In summary, a well-planned nutrition strategy that includes increased calorie intake, a balanced macronutrient diet, adequate hydration, and monitoring of key micronutrients like iron is crucial for mountain athletes. By being intentional with their high-altitude nutrition plan, athletes can mitigate the risks associated with altitude training and maximize their performance benefits.
If you are planning a high-altitude training block or race, it is worth consulting with a dietitian or using reliable nutrition resources to develop a customized plan. With the right nutritional approach, you can ensure that your body is well-prepared to handle the challenges of training at altitude.
